The context

Oracle Health operates inside one of the most consequential federal healthcare environments in the country. When something doesn't work in a VA system — when a ticket is closed without resolution, classified incorrectly, or escalated to the wrong owner — that gap eventually surfaces somewhere it matters. Operational reporting isn't a back-office function in this context. It's how leadership sees what the front line is actually doing.

My role exists at the seam between raw ticket data and the people who need to act on it. I own the recurring analytics, audits, and leadership-facing reports that translate operational performance into decisions.

What I own

Monthly SLA compliance review

Manually audit incident tickets each month to validate closure accuracy and classification — request vs. restoration, probable cause, product, status reason. The review identifies process gaps before they propagate into leadership reporting.

"No Client Response" closure audits

Review tickets closed under "No Client Response" status to confirm that the required communication and process steps were followed before closure. Findings are escalated to the responsible teams for resolution.

Leadership-facing reporting and decks

Populate org-wide operational health reports and executive decks used by Oracle and VA leadership. The work requires accuracy under high visibility — and the discipline to translate detailed ticket-level findings into a summary an executive can read in two minutes.

Raw data pulls and ad hoc analytics

Pull from ticketing systems and BI tools to answer urgent leadership questions — backlog aging, ownership gaps, classification trends, defect patterns. The work shifts constantly based on what's surfacing operationally.

VA stakeholder liaison

Twice-weekly calls and ongoing email coordination with VA leadership on ticket hygiene and case management. The role is part analyst, part translator — turning Oracle-side operational details into something VA stakeholders can use without losing the substance.

How I think about the work

Three principles shape how I approach this role:

  • Reporting is governance. Every classification I validate, every closure I question, every label I push back on — that's data governance happening at the ticket level. If the inputs aren't right, the leadership report isn't right, and the decisions aren't right.
  • Accuracy compounds at scale. A small classification error in one ticket is noise. The same error repeated across hundreds of tickets per month is a trend leadership will act on — incorrectly. I double-check and triple-check work that's going to executives because the cost of being wrong is high and the cost of being slow is lower than people think.
  • Translation is the job. Most analytical findings die not because they're wrong, but because they're not made legible to the people who need to act on them. The best report answers the question before anyone has to ask it.

What the role has confirmed

A few observations that aren't obvious from the outside:

  • Operational data is usually not the bottleneck. The data exists. The problem is almost always the process around how it's labeled, closed, and routed. The fix for a reporting problem is often a clearer checklist, not a better algorithm.
  • Stakeholders trust people who say "I don't know" out loud. The worst thing an analyst can do is invent a clean answer when there isn't one. I've built more trust by saying "let me confirm and come back to you" than by guessing well.
  • Process improvement is downstream of being useful. The reason I get to recommend process changes is that people know I'll answer their data question accurately and quickly first. Influence in operations is earned at the ticket level.
