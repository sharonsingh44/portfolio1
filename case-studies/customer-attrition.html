Case study ii · Applied ML · Customer retention

Predicting customer attrition across a 10,000-account portfolio

Comparing five classifiers to flag at-risk customers before they churned — and turning the winning model's feature importances into a concrete retention playbook.

Role

Solo

Stack

R

Models

5 compared

Winner

XGBoost

The problem

A corporation needed to identify customers at high risk of leaving — and, just as importantly, understand why they were leaving. Attrition was draining revenue and degrading service reputation, but the existing process was reactive: customers were already gone by the time anyone noticed. The ask was twofold: build a model that could predict attrition probability, and surface the customer features that drove it so the business could act before churn happened.

Data & approach

~10,000 customer records spanning demographics, account information, transaction history, and engagement signals. Target variable was Attrition_Flag (binary: existing vs. attrited).

Pipeline

Data cleansing → outlier handling (IQR + capping) → one-hot encoding → normalization → 80/20 train/test split → stratified sampling → 5-model comparison → hyperparameter tuning via cross-validation → ROC/AUC evaluation against a null baseline

The work

I deliberately compared a range of model families rather than reaching straight for the most complex option — partly to confirm the simpler models couldn't do the job, and partly so the final write-up could explain why XGBoost was chosen, not just that it won.

XGBoost winner97.14%0.991

Logistic regression actually held up surprisingly well as a baseline — useful, because it meant the simpler interpretable model was already explaining most of the signal. The tree-based models (Random Forest and XGBoost) earned their complexity by handling the non-linear interactions and class imbalance more cleanly. The gap between train and test accuracy on XGBoost (0.99 vs. 0.97) suggested it was generalizing rather than memorizing.

The more interesting work was after the model selection. Once XGBoost was the winner, the feature importance plot told a story:

Top drivers of attrition

  • Total transaction count. Drops in transaction frequency were the strongest churn signal.
  • Average open-to-buy ratio. Lower available credit correlated with higher attrition.
  • Total transaction amount. Higher-spend customers were less likely to leave.
  • Credit limit. Higher limits, lower churn risk.
  • Gender. Male customers showed measurably different retention patterns.

Translating signal into action

A 99% accuracy number is not useful on its own — what matters is whether the business can act on it. I closed the project by mapping each top feature to a concrete retention lever:

  • Declining transaction count → trigger a re-engagement campaign before the customer goes fully dormant
  • Low open-to-buy ratio → proactively review for credit limit increases on eligible accounts
  • High-spend customers → invest in loyalty programs that protect the most profitable segment
  • Demographic patterns → run a controlled test on segment-specific messaging
  • Resource allocation → score the book monthly and prioritize outreach by attrition probability, not gut feel

What I'd do differently

An AUC of 0.991 on a real-world business problem should always raise an eyebrow — including my own. A few things I would push harder on in a production version:

  • Audit for target leakage. Some predictors — like change in transaction count Q4 vs. Q1 — may carry post-churn information depending on how the snapshot was taken. I'd validate the timing of every feature against the churn event.
  • Address class imbalance more rigorously. The attrited class was the minority. SMOTE or class weighting could change which model actually wins on the metric that matters (recall on churners, not overall accuracy).
  • Optimize for the business metric, not accuracy. A false negative (missing a churner) costs more than a false positive (over-contacting a happy customer). I'd retrain optimizing for recall on the positive class and revisit the threshold.
  • Validate on a holdout time window. An 80/20 random split is fine for coursework. In production, I'd train on months 1–9 and test on months 10–12 to confirm the model generalizes forward in time.
