Balancing data privacy and utility in the digital age

A position paper framed as expert testimony to the U.S. Subcommittee on Innovation, Data, and Commerce — arguing that strong privacy and meaningful data utility are not opposites, and that organizations can pursue both through strategic governance.

The thesis

Privacy and utility are not opposites. Organizations can protect individual rights while unlocking the value of data — provided they adopt strategic governance frameworks, leverage privacy-preserving technologies, and align practices with evolving legal and ethical standards.

The conventional framing — that privacy controls and data utility trade off against each other — is outdated. It made sense in a world before federated learning, differential privacy, and modern governance frameworks. It does not survive contact with the current technical and legal landscape.

The argument, in four moves

i. The stakes

Healthcare and commercial data both create real value (predictive medicine, fraud detection, personalization) and real risk (the 2015 Anthem breach exposed 80M records; Cambridge Analytica showed how unchecked utility erodes democracy). Neither side of the tradeoff is theoretical.

ii. The legal landscape

HIPAA, CCPA, GDPR, and China's PIPL each balance privacy and utility differently — individual rights in the West, state sovereignty in China. The U.S. patchwork of state laws creates compliance burden without sufficient protection. A federal privacy law would harmonize standards while strengthening individual rights.

iii. The technical tools

Differential privacy adds calibrated noise to preserve aggregate insights while protecting individuals. Federated learning trains models across decentralized data without centralizing sensitive records. Privacy-by-design embeds protections into systems from the start. These methods don't eliminate the privacy-utility tradeoff — they relocate it.

iv. The path forward

A risk-based, proportionate federal framework + privacy-by-design as organizational standard + public-private partnership on emerging privacy-preserving tech. Skeptics argue regulation slows innovation; the better-designed regulations actually focus it.

Why this matters to my analytics work

I do not treat governance as separate from analytics. The same instincts that make a model trustworthy — knowing where the data came from, what it's allowed to be used for, who's accountable for it — make an organization trustworthy.

In my day job at Oracle Health, I work in federal healthcare environments where HIPAA, data lineage, ticket hygiene, and reporting integrity are not policy abstractions — they're how the work gets done. Writing this paper sharpened how I think about my own role: the operational decisions I make about data quality and process adherence are governance decisions. This case study exists on the portfolio because I want that connection visible to anyone hiring me.
