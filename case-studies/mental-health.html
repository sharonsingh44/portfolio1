Predicting mental health service utilization in young adult men

Why does a vulnerable population avoid help — and can a model identify who's most likely to fall through the cracks? Led a three-person team through a project that started with a near-failing model and ended with a 30% AUC improvement and honest framing of what's still unsolved.

Role

Team lead

Team size

3 people

Domain

Public health

AUC lift

+30%

The problem

In 2021, only 40% of men with mental illness received treatment, compared to 52% of women. Young adult men aged 18–25 are one of the most underserved populations in mental health care — barriers include cultural norms, stigma, and structural access gaps. Public health interventions can only target what they can identify.

Our research question: Among U.S. men aged 18–25 reporting mental health challenges, what demographic, socioeconomic, and geographic factors predict whether they utilize professional mental health services?

Data & approach

Merged three public datasets from the Medical Expenditure Panel Survey (HC-244, HC-266, HC-277) using person ID as the join key. After filtering to U.S. men aged 18–25 reporting mental health challenges, we had a clean cohort with predictors spanning age, race, education, poverty category, total personal income, insurance coverage, and region.

Pipeline

Dataset merge (HC-244 + HC-266 + HC-277) → cohort filter → NA handling → feature engineering (age brackets, visit counts) → 75/25 stratified split → Random Forest regression → variable importance pruning → reframed target → Random Forest classification → ROC/AUC + confusion matrix

The work, in two acts

Act i. The first model failed honestly

I started by treating service utilization as a continuous outcome — modeling the number of mental health visits with a Random Forest regression. The variable importance plot identified total personal income, age, and education years as top predictors, which let us prune the noisier features and build a cleaner Model 1.

Model 1's AUC came back at 0.5357. Barely above the 0.5 random-guess baseline.

A lot of teams would have buried this. We flagged it, diagnosed it, and treated it as the more interesting finding: the model couldn't distinguish service users from non-users because the target variable was wrong, the class imbalance was severe, and the regression framing was forcing the model to fit something noisier than it needed to.

Act ii. The fix

Three changes to Model 2:

  • Reframed the target from continuous visit count to a binary classification (used services: yes/no)
  • Collapsed the perceived mental health score from 5 categories into 2 (good: 1–3, poor: 4–5) to reduce noise and concentrate signal
  • Pruned the feature set using the importance plot from Model 1 — keeping only predictors that contributed to MSE reduction and node purity

AUC progression

Random baseline 0.500
Model 1 — regression on visit count 0.536
Model 2 — binary classification, pruned features 0.697

What the model found

Total personal income, age, and education years were the strongest predictors of whether a young man with mental health challenges actually accessed care. Insurance coverage and poverty category also carried signal — pointing to access barriers that are structural, not just cultural.

For a public health team, the practical translation is that targeted outreach to low-income, lower-education segments — and ensuring insurance pathways are clear — would likely move the needle more than broad anti-stigma messaging alone.

What's still unsolved

An AUC of 0.70 is a real improvement, but it is not a deployable model. The confusion matrix showed a class error of 0.94 on the "Poor" health class — meaning the model was missing almost everyone we most needed to find. Honest next steps:

  • Apply SMOTE or class weighting to address the imbalance directly rather than working around it
  • Optimize for recall on the minority class, not overall accuracy — a public health screening tool should over-flag, not under-flag
  • Compare against XGBoost and gradient boosting variants with hyperparameter tuning, not just Random Forest
  • Investigate the data itself. A 0.94 false negative rate suggests the signal may not be in this dataset at all — the next step might be richer features (geographic stigma proxies, social network data) rather than a better algorithm

Reflection

The honest takeaway wasn't the +30% AUC lift — it was the discipline of letting Model 1 fail visibly, diagnosing why, and writing the limitations of Model 2 with the same rigor as its results.

Real-world models on real-world social data rarely produce 95% accuracy. The work is in deciding when the model is good enough to inform a decision, and when it's still telling you to go back to the data.
